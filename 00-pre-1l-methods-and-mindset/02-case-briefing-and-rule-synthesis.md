# Case Briefing And Rule Synthesis

Briefing forces an opinion into a usable shape. Synthesis combines many briefs into the actual law. The first is mechanical; the second is the real skill.

## The Brief

Use the [case-brief template](../templates/case-brief-template.md). Keep each field short.

- `citation`
- `procedural posture`
- `key facts` — only the legally operative ones
- `issue` — phrased as a yes/no legal question
- `rule` — in your own words
- `holding` — the court's answer
- `reasoning` — why, in two or three sentences
- `one-sentence exam takeaway`

A good brief is shorter than the opinion by an order of magnitude. If your brief is half the length of the case, you are transcribing, not briefing.

## Full Brief Versus Book Brief

- `full brief` — a separate written brief; use this early while the skill is forming
- `book brief` — margin annotations and highlighting in the text itself; use this once you can reliably spot issue, rule, and holding on the fly

Move from full briefs to book briefs as you gain speed. Do not start with book briefing.

## From Briefs To Rules: Synthesis

A single case rarely gives you the whole rule. Doctrine is built by reading several cases together and extracting the rule that explains all of them.

### The synthesis move

1. line up the cases on one issue
2. note the `outcome` of each
3. ask what fact difference explains why the outcomes differ
4. state the rule that predicts all of the outcomes
5. note the open question the cases have not yet resolved

### Example shape (negligence duty)

- case A: liability, because the defendant created the risk
- case B: no liability, because the harm was unforeseeable
- synthesized rule: a defendant owes a duty of care for `foreseeable` risks to `foreseeable` plaintiffs

You are reverse-engineering the rule from results. That is exactly what a court does with precedent and what an exam rewards.

## Majority And Minority Rules

When cases split, capture both:

- the `majority rule` most jurisdictions follow
- the `minority rule`, and which value it protects

Exams love the fact pattern that comes out differently under each.

## Deliverable

For each subject, keep one growing `rule sheet`: one-line synthesized rules, no case summaries. This sheet becomes the spine of your outline in [03-how-to-outline-and-build-attack-sheets.md](./03-how-to-outline-and-build-attack-sheets.md).
