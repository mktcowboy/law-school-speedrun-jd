# How To Read A Legal Opinion

The judicial opinion is the basic unit of common-law study. If you can read one well, every casebook in this plan opens up.

## What An Opinion Is

An opinion is a court explaining `who won`, `on what legal question`, and `why`. Everything in it serves one of those three jobs. Your reading should sort the text into those buckets.

## Anatomy Of An Opinion

- `caption` — parties, court, date, citation
- `procedural posture` — how the case arrived (appeal from what ruling, on what motion)
- `facts` — what happened, and what the court treats as legally relevant
- `issue` — the precise legal question being decided
- `holding` — the court's answer to that question
- `reasoning` — the rule plus the court's application of it
- `disposition` — what happens now (affirmed, reversed, remanded)
- `concurrences and dissents` — separate opinions; not binding, but often where the doctrine is stress-tested

## Read In Two Passes

### First pass — orientation

- read the first paragraph and the last
- find the disposition
- ask: what question did the court have to answer to get there

### Second pass — extraction

- mark the operative `rule`
- mark the `facts` that triggered the rule
- mark the step where the court applies rule to facts
- ignore color, history, and side issues on this pass

## Holding Versus Dicta

- `holding` — the rule necessary to decide the actual dispute; binding
- `dicta` — statements the court did not need to reach the result; persuasive at most

Most casebook confusion comes from treating dicta as if it were holding. Always ask whether the court needed the statement to decide the case.

## Procedural Posture Controls Everything

The same facts produce different questions depending on posture:

- on a `12(b)(6)` motion, the court assumes the complaint's facts are true
- on `summary judgment`, the court asks whether any material fact is genuinely disputed
- on `appeal`, the court reviews a specific ruling under a specific standard of review

Never state a holding without knowing the posture it came from.

## Majority, Concurrence, Dissent

- the `majority` is the law
- a `concurrence` agrees with the result but not the reasoning — watch for a narrower rule
- a `dissent` shows the strongest argument the other way, which is exactly what an exam tests

## What To Carry Out Of Every Opinion

- one-sentence `rule`
- the `fact` that made the rule apply
- where this case sits relative to the cases around it

If you cannot produce those three things, run the second pass again.
