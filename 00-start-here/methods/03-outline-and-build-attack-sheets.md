# How To Outline And Build Attack Sheets

The outline is where reading turns into knowledge you can use. In a real law school, the outline — not the casebook — is what students study from for the exam.

## What An Outline Is

A `subject outline` is your own reorganization of the course around `doctrine`, not around cases or reading order. Cases become evidence for rules; the rules are the structure.

- organized by issue and sub-issue
- `5 to 15 pages`
- rules in your own words, each tagged with the case or statute that supports it
- built and updated `as you read`, not the week before an exam

## Outline Versus Attack Outline

- `outline` — the full reference; everything you might need to look up
- `attack outline` — `1 to 2 pages`; the issue-spotting checklist you would actually use under time pressure

The attack outline is the higher-value artifact. It is a sequence of triggers and tests, not prose.

### Attack-outline shape (per issue)

- trigger: what fact pattern raises this issue
- rule: the elements or test, in order
- splits: majority vs. minority, if tested
- common trap: the mistake to avoid

## How To Build One

1. start from your synthesized `rule sheet`
2. group rules into the subject's major buckets (for torts: intentional torts, negligence, defenses, damages)
3. under each bucket, lay out the test element by element
4. collapse each element into a trigger you can recognize in a fact pattern
5. cut everything you would not reach for during a timed answer

## Doctrine-First, Not Case-First

The single most common outlining mistake is organizing by case. Cases are interchangeable evidence for rules. Organize by the `rule`, then hang the cases underneath.

## Common Mistakes

- copying the casebook table of contents and calling it an outline
- writing long case summaries instead of one-line rules
- building the outline at the end instead of continuously
- never compressing the full outline into an attack outline

## Deliverable Standard

A subject is not "done" until you have:

- a `5 to 15 page` outline
- a `1 to 2 page` attack outline
- a list of rules you still cannot recall cold
