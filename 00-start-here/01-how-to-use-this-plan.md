# How To Use This Plan

This plan is built around content mastery first and scheduling second.

The main idea is simple:

- follow the traditional `1L -> 2L -> 3L` sequence
- study one subject deeply enough to outline it
- keep producing written work as you go
- use the optional speedrun only if you want compression guidance

## Default Cadence

The default version is `not` week-locked.

You can move subject by subject at the pace that fits your life:

- fast track: `1 to 2 weeks` per major subject
- steady track: `2 to 4 weeks` per major subject
- deep track: `1 month or more` per major subject

The important thing is not the calendar. It is whether you can explain and apply the doctrine.

## What You Are Actually Training

- how to read a judicial opinion
- how to extract a legal rule from messy facts
- how to read statutes and procedural rules
- how to compare common-law reasoning to code-based reasoning
- how to write short, exam-style legal analysis

## The Minimum Standard For Finishing A Subject

Do not call a subject complete unless you have all of the following:

- a `5 to 15 page` personal outline
- a `1 to 2 page` attack outline
- at least `3 written case briefs` or statute briefs
- at least `2 short issue-spotting answers`
- a list of rules you still cannot recall cold

## Time Budget Options

### Content-first track

- study until the subject clicks
- advance when your outline and writing show real understanding

### Busy-professional track

- `8 to 15 hours` per week
- move more slowly, but keep the same written deliverables

### Optional speedrun track

- use the `99-optional-speedrun` folder
- keep the same deliverables, just on a compressed timeline

## What To Skip

Skip these traps early:

- endless law school advice videos
- buying every supplement in sight
- reading every note case in full
- obsessing over minority rules before you know majority structure
- doing bar questions before you know the core vocabulary of the subject

## Order Of Operations

1. Read the year and subject overview.
2. Do the primary reading.
3. Build your own rule map.
4. Test yourself with a short hypo or issue spotter.
5. Update the attack outline.
6. Write the subject review.

## If You Want To Go Even Harder

Add:

- one oral explanation of each major doctrine as if teaching a classmate
- one closed-book rewrite of each week's attack outline
- one mixed-subject essay every time you finish a major subject

## Traditional JD Reality Check

In a real law school, `1L` is where students learn legal method and core private/public law subjects. `2L` is where doctrine expands and specialization begins. `3L` is where practice, writing, clinics, and bar-bridge work matter more.

That is the structure this folder now follows.

# 1L Foundation Year

`1L` is the year that rewires how you read and reason.

This section should be your default starting point even if your long-term goal is to move quickly. The subjects here build the vocabulary that makes later statutory and practice-heavy courses intelligible.

## Typical 1L Subjects

- civil procedure
- torts
- contracts
- property
- criminal law
- constitutional law
- legal research and writing

## Recommended Study Order

If you want a traditional-but-practical flow, use:

1. civil procedure
2. torts
3. contracts
4. property
5. criminal law
6. constitutional law
7. legal research and writing throughout

## What 1L Should Produce

By the end of this section, you want:

- case-reading fluency
- working command of common-law reasoning
- comfort with procedural rules
- the habit of building outlines and attack sheets
- enough writing practice to move beyond passive reading

## Books To Prioritize In 1L

- `Getting to Maybe`
- `A Short and Happy Guide to Acing Your 1L Year`
- one subject-specific supplement only where you actually need help

# 2L Doctrinal Depth And Codes

`2L` is where legal education usually becomes more specialized, more code-driven, and more professionalized.

This is the right time to push deeper into procedure, evidence, commercial law, regulation, and the law of organizations.

## Typical 2L Subjects

- evidence
- criminal procedure
- business associations
- commercial law, UCC, and secured transactions
- administrative law
- professional responsibility

## What Changes In 2L

Compared with `1L`, you should expect more:

- statutory reading
- defined-term dependency
- regulatory structure
- doctrine built around tests and procedural posture
- practical career choices such as journals, clinics, and summer work

## What 2L Should Produce

- comfort reading black-letter rules directly
- better synthesis across cases and codes
- more precise writing under doctrinal constraints
- a clearer sense of your practice interests

## Practical Overlay

Do not treat this section as purely academic. It is where people often choose:

- journal versus non-journal writing paths
- litigation versus transactional emphasis
- summer employer targeting
- clinic or externship direction for `3L`

# 3L Practice, Synthesis, And Launch

`3L` is where the curriculum should stop pretending doctrine alone is enough.

This stage is about integrating what you know with actual practice systems, professional judgment, writing, and bar/career launch planning.

## Typical 3L Priorities

- clinics and externships
- trial advocacy and drafting
- advanced seminars or capstones
- bar-aligned synthesis
- job transition and practice readiness

## What 3L Should Produce

- cleaner writing
- stronger judgment about process and strategy
- better issue-spotting across subjects
- a more realistic understanding of how lawyers actually work

## Do Not Waste 3L

The self-study version of `3L` should not be a pile of random electives.

Use it to:

- sharpen writing
- simulate practice
- do capstone synthesis
- prepare for the format of the exam or job transition ahead

# Optional Speedrun Layout

This folder preserves the original compressed structure for people who want a strict timeline.

It is now secondary, not primary.

## Who Should Use This

- people who already understand the purpose of the core subjects
- people building a crash-course map before deeper study
- people who want a time-boxed boot camp after finishing the main year-based curriculum

## What It Does Well

- forces momentum
- groups related doctrines efficiently
- gives a hard-edged study cadence

## What It Does Poorly

- it can make people think finishing a week means mastering a subject
- it is less faithful to the ordinary `1L/2L/3L` sequencing of law school
- it encourages compression before comprehension if used too early

## Recommended Use

Use the traditional curriculum first:

- [01-1l-foundation-year](../01-1l-foundation-year/README.md)
