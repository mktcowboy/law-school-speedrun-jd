# Study System And Deliverables

Law school skill begins with reading well. The rest of the system exists to make the reading stick.

## Reading-First Progression

For most subjects, use this order:

1. orientation source
2. main open casebook or textbook
3. controlling rule, statute, or constitutional text
4. short synthesis notes in your own words

Do not start with supplements unless the free pass has already failed.

## Core Workflow For Every Reading Block

1. Read for `structure`, not trivia.
2. Identify the issue.
3. State the rule in your own words.
4. Note the facts that made the rule matter.
5. Capture the holding and reasoning.
6. Add the rule to your outline immediately.

## What To Do After A Reading Session

- compress the section into `3 to 7` rules
- note any unfamiliar doctrine or vocabulary
- mark what should be reread slowly
- move forward before chasing every side issue

## Deliverables You Should Maintain

These are secondary to reading, but they keep the reading from evaporating.

### 1. Subject outline

- `5 to 15 pages`
- organized by doctrine
- updated daily

### 2. Attack outline

- `1 to 2 pages`
- what you would actually use under time pressure

### 3. Rule sheet

- one-line rules only
- no long case summaries

### 4. Error log

- what you missed
- why you missed it
- what rule fixes the miss

### 5. Reading log

- source
- chapter or rule set
- cases or provisions covered
- questions to revisit

### 6. Writing file

- short IRACs
- comparisons between majority/minority rules
- policy notes only when they help analysis

## Marking A Subject Done

Use one [subject progress checklist](../templates/subject-progress-checklist-template.md) per subject so "done" means something concrete. A subject is not finished because you read it once; it is finished when the reading, synthesis, writing, and canon-check boxes are all real.

## The Three Reading Modes

### Case mode

Use when reading common-law subjects:

- torts
- contracts
- property
- constitutional law
- evidence

### Statute mode

Use when reading code-heavy subjects:

- criminal law
- UCC
- administrative law
- professional responsibility

### Procedure mode

Use when reading:

- FRCP
- FRE
- criminal procedure
- agency review frameworks

## What Your Subject Review Should Ask

- What were the five most important rules in this subject?
- Where do I still confuse elements, standards, and defenses?
- What would a professor most likely test?
- Which cases or statutes changed how I think about the subject?
- Can I explain the week's doctrine out loud without my notes?

## Memorization Rule

Memorize only after understanding.

What to memorize:

- elements
- standards
- burdens
- tests
- rule triggers

What not to memorize first:

- colorful facts from famous cases
- long dicta
- isolated exceptions before the baseline rule
