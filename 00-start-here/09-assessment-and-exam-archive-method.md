# Assessment And Exam-Archive Method

The hardest part of self-study is the missing feedback loop. There is no cold call, no graded memo, no curve. This file builds the replacement: a repeatable cycle of timed writing measured against real questions and model answers.

This is the single most important habit for closing the gap with a classroom `1L`.

## Why You Need This

Reading and outlining feel like progress but do not test retrieval or application. You only learn what you actually know by writing answers under time pressure and comparing them to a standard.

## The Core Loop

1. pick one timed question
2. write a full answer under the clock, closed-book after your first read
3. compare against a model answer or grading rubric
4. log every miss in an `error log`: what you missed, why, and the rule that fixes it
5. fold the fix back into your attack outline
6. repeat on the same issue type until the miss stops recurring

The `error log` is the engine. A miss you record and re-test is worth more than ten passively reread pages.

## Where To Find Questions

- `NCBE NextGen sample questions` — official, format-matched to the exam ahead: <https://www.ncbex.org/exams/nextgen/sample-questions>
- `NextGen Bar Exam` updates and released materials: <https://nextgenbarexam.ncbex.org/>
- `CALI` lessons — guided, auto-scored practice by subject (school authorization code required): <https://www.cali.org/>
- public `law-school exam archives` — many professors post past exams and model answers; search a specific school's exam archive or `"[subject] law school practice exam"`
- your own `issue spotters` built from the fact patterns in your casebook

## Choosing A Question

- early in a subject: short single-issue hypos
- mid-subject: multi-issue essays from one doctrinal area
- end of subject: a full cross-issue exam-style question
- after several subjects: a mixed-subject question that forces issue-spotting across fields

## Writing Under Time

Use the method in [00-pre-1l-methods-and-mindset/04-how-to-write-short-irac-analysis.md](../00-pre-1l-methods-and-mindset/04-how-to-write-short-irac-analysis.md):

- budget time per question first
- spot and order issues before writing
- tight `IRAC` per issue, application-heavy, both sides

## Grading Yourself

Without a professor, grade against structure:

- did I spot every issue the facts raised
- did I state each rule correctly
- did I apply facts to each element, both ways
- did I reach a conclusion on each issue
- where did the model answer go that I did not

Score the `process`, not just the bottom line.

## Cadence

- one timed question after finishing each major doctrinal bucket
- one full subject question when the subject's outline is complete
- one mixed-subject question after each year-stage of the plan
- review the `error log` weekly; retire a miss only after you get it right cold
