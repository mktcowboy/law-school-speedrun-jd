# Conflict Of Laws

## Why It Is Here

Conflict of laws — choice of law, jurisdiction, and the recognition of judgments across state lines — is a `legacy MEE` bar subject that the `NextGen UBE` drops from foundational testing. It also runs underneath `civil procedure` (`Erie`) and `constitutional law` (Full Faith and Credit). Build it for a legacy or transition jurisdiction, or to deepen procedure. Confirm your jurisdiction first.

## Master These First

- the three classic choice-of-law methods: the `First Restatement` vested-rights / lex loci approach, interest analysis, and the `Second Restatement` most-significant-relationship test
- domicile as the connecting factor
- the `Erie` doctrine and `Klaxon`: which state's law a federal court in diversity applies
- Full Faith and Credit and the recognition of sister-state judgments
- the constitutional limits on choice of law
- characterization, `renvoi`, and the public-policy escape devices

## Landmark Cases

- `Erie R. Co. v. Tompkins` — state substantive law in federal diversity cases
- `Klaxon Co. v. Stentor Electric` — a federal court applies the forum state's choice-of-law rules
- `Babcock v. Jackson` — the turn to interest analysis and the most-significant-relationship approach
- `Allstate Insurance Co. v. Hague` — the constitutional floor for applying forum law

## Common Exam Traps

- name the method before you apply it; `lex loci` and interest analysis can reach opposite results on the same facts
- in federal court, run `Erie` then `Klaxon` — apply the forum state's choice-of-law rule, not your own
- keep `jurisdiction` (can the forum hear it), `choice of law` (whose law applies), and `judgments` (must another state honor it) as three separate questions
- Full Faith and Credit is near-absolute for `judgments` but weaker for `laws`
- watch the escape devices — characterization, `renvoi`, and public policy — used to avoid an unwanted result
