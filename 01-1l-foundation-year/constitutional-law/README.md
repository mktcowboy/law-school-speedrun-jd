# Constitutional Law

## Why It Belongs In 1L

Constitutional law gives structure to public power and rights analysis. It belongs in the foundation sequence.

## Con Law I Versus Con Law II

Most `T10/Ivy` programs split this subject across two courses, and you should study it that way even when one casebook covers both:

- `Con Law I — Structure` (usually `1L`): judicial review, federalism and the Commerce Clause, separation of powers, the anti-commandeering rule
- `Con Law II — Rights and Liberties` (often a later `2L` continuation): equal protection, substantive and procedural due process, and the First Amendment

Build the structure half first; rights doctrine assumes you already know judicial review, state action, and the tiers of scrutiny.

## Master These First

### Structure (Con Law I)

- judicial review as background architecture
- federalism and the Commerce Clause, from `Gibbons` through `Lopez`
- separation of powers and the `Youngstown` framework
- the anti-commandeering principle
- state action

### Rights (Con Law II)

- the levels of scrutiny and who bears the burden at each
- equal protection, including the discriminatory-intent requirement
- substantive due process and the privacy/liberty line
- First Amendment speech: incitement, defamation, content neutrality
- First Amendment religion: Free Exercise and Establishment basics

## Common Exam Traps

- clear the threshold (standing, state action) before reaching the merits
- pick the correct tier of scrutiny and state who bears the burden at that tier
- for equal protection, separate disparate impact from the discriminatory intent `Washington v. Davis` requires
- keep substantive due process (fundamental rights) distinct from procedural due process (notice and hearing)
- distinguish facial from as-applied challenges, and content-based from content-neutral speech regulation
- in religion problems, separate Free Exercise (`Smith`) from Establishment analysis
- do not blur Equal Protection, Due Process, and the Dormant Commerce Clause — each has its own framework
