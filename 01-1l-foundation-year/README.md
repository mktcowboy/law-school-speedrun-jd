# 1L Foundation Year

`1L` is the year that rewires how you read and reason.

This section should be your default starting point even if your long-term goal is to move quickly. The subjects here build the vocabulary that makes later statutory and practice-heavy courses intelligible.

You can complete a strong first pass through this year almost entirely with free resources.

This part of the curriculum should feel overwhelmingly like `reading`, not simulation. The main job of `1L` is to read enough cases, rules, and short explanatory materials to understand how legal doctrine is built.

## Typical 1L Subjects

- civil procedure
- torts
- contracts
- property
- criminal law
- constitutional law
- legal research and writing

## Recommended Study Order

If you want a traditional-but-practical flow, use:

1. civil procedure
2. torts
3. contracts
4. property
5. criminal law
6. constitutional law
7. legal research and writing throughout

## Reading-First Strategy For 1L

For each subject, use a four-step sequence:

1. read a short orientation source such as `Wex` or a `CALI subject outline`
2. read the main open casebook or free text
3. read the most important primary-law materials
4. compress the reading into your own outline

The key mistake to avoid is jumping too quickly into practice problems before you have read enough doctrine to recognize the issues.

## What To Read Closely In 1L

- read `cases` closely in torts, contracts, property, criminal law, and constitutional law
- read `rules` closely in civil procedure
- read `citations, source hierarchy, and research workflow` closely in legal research and writing

## What A Good First Pass Looks Like

A good first pass through a `1L` subject usually means:

- you have read one real open casebook or substantial free text
- you have read the most important rule set or constitutional provisions behind the subject
- you can explain the subject's major doctrinal buckets without looking at your notes
- you know which parts need a second, slower pass

## What 1L Should Produce

By the end of this section, you want:

- case-reading fluency
- working command of common-law reasoning
- comfort with procedural rules
- the habit of building outlines and attack sheets
- enough writing practice to move beyond passive reading

## Books To Prioritize In 1L

- default to `H2O`, `CALI`, `LII`, `Oyez`, and the free citation tools first
- consider `Getting to Maybe` only if you want an optional paid exam-method book
- add a subject-specific supplement only where you actually need help
