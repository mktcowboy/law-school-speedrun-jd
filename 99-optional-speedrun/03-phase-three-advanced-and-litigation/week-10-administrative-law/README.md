# Week 10: Administrative Law

## Focus

Study the law of the administrative state: agencies, delegation, rulemaking, adjudication, and judicial review.

## Outcomes

By the end of the week, you should be able to explain:

- why agencies exist
- the difference between rulemaking and adjudication
- where agency power comes from
- the Administrative Procedure Act at a high level
- standards and posture of judicial review

## Primary Reading

- Harvard-hosted H2O Administrative Law: <https://opencasebook.org/casebooks/8962-administrative-law/>
- Penn The Regulatory Review: <https://www.theregreview.org/>
- Harvard Statutes guide and Stanford Federal Legislative History for the statutory-interpretation layer: <https://guides.library.harvard.edu/law/statutes> and <https://guides.law.stanford.edu/fedleghistory>
- eCFR portal: <https://www.ecfr.gov/>
- Federal Register: <https://www.federalregister.gov/>
- GovInfo: <https://www.govinfo.gov/>

Cases and concepts to know:

- `Loper Bright` (2024) — courts interpret statutes de novo; `Chevron` is overruled, so know it only as background
- `Skidmore` — persuasive-weight deference
- `West Virginia v. EPA` (2022) — the major-questions doctrine
- arbitrary and capricious review
- notice-and-comment rulemaking
- statutory interpretation: textualism, purposivism, and the canons — the `leg-reg` companion to administrative law

## What To Build

- one map of `statute -> agency -> regulation -> adjudication -> judicial review`
- one APA terminology sheet
- one standards-of-review chart
- one agency action checklist

## Suggested Daily Split

- Day 1: agency structure and delegation background
- Day 2: rulemaking
- Day 3: adjudication and procedure
- Day 4: judicial review
- Day 5: deference doctrines and current landscape
- Day 6: synthesis writing

## Writing Assignment

- Explain how a federal statute can turn into a binding regulation.
- Analyze a simple agency-review problem.

## If You Buy One Supplement

- only if administrative law becomes a recurring professional need

## Week Exit Standard

You can explain how the legal system governs the people who govern.
